PERFECT TENSE: REDUPLICATION

Reduplication has three forms:—

(1) If the stems begin with a consonant (except ρ), followed by a vowel or by λ, μ, ν, ρ (liquids and nasals), prefix the first consonant with ε, adding the proper endings.

λύ-ω λέ-λυ-κα
θραύ-ω τέ-θραυ-κα

N.B.—Aspirates (χ, θ, φ) are reduplicated by their unvoiced sounds, κ τ, π.

(2) If the stem begins with two or more consonants (except as above), or with a double consonant (ζ, ξ, ψ), or with ρ, prefix ἐ- (doubling the ρ).

πταί-ω ἔ-πται-κα
ψαύ-ω ἔ-ψαυ-κα
ῥίπ-τω ἔ-ρρῑφα

(3) If the stem begins with a vowel, augment as for the imperfect:—

ἄγγελλω ἤγγελκα

Contracted verbs lengthen the stem-character as for future and aorist (α-stems and ε-stems have η, ο-stems ω):—

τε-τίμη-κα πε-ποίη-κα δε-δούλω-κα

There are two Perfects, as two Aorists: one, called the Strong or Second Perfect, being formed straight from the Strong Root. Very few verbs have both, and when they do, the two usually differ in meaning: the strong perfect being intransitive.

Example:—

The principal parts of a Verb are: Present, Future, Aorist, and Perfect—λύω, λύσω, ἔλῡσα, λέλυκα.

The paradigm of a given tense consists of the first form of each mood in the conjugate scheme, as: ἔλυσα λῦσον λύσω λύσαιμι λῦσαι λύσας. These are also called Cognate Forms.

OTHER STEMS OF THE THIRD DECLENSION

The declension of other stems is similar; the genitive case will show whether the vowel be long or short. Thus:—

ὁ θήρ ‘beast,’ θηρός, θηρσί(ν)—(ρ-stem).
ὁ ῥήτωρ ‘orator,’ ῥήτορος, ῥήτορσι(ν)—(ρ-stem).
ὁ ἅλς ‘salt,’ ἁλός, ἁλσί(ν)—(the only λ-stem).
ὁ, ἡ αἴξ ‘goat,’ αἰγός, αἰξί(ν)—(guttural stem).
ὁ γύψ ‘vulture,’ γῡπός, γῡψί(ν)—(labial stem).
ὁ, ἡ φυγάς ‘fugitive,’ φυγάδος, φυγάσι(ν)—(dental stem).
ὁ θής ‘serf,’ θητός, θησί(ν)—(dental stem). Like this are declined all the abstract nouns in -της formed from adjectives: as ἡ χρηστότης ‘honesty,’ χρηστότητος.
